What Is ROI?

Return on investment (ROI) is a performance measure used to evaluate the efficiency or profitability of an investment. It expresses the gain or loss generated relative to the amount of money invested. ROI is widely used in finance, real estate, marketing, and business planning because it provides a simple, comparable metric across different types of investments.

When ROI Can Be Misleading

ROI has several limitations. It does not account for the time value of moneyâ€”$100 today is worth more than $100 in five years. It often excludes transaction costs, taxes, and ongoing fees, which can significantly reduce actual returns. ROI can also be manipulated by choosing selective time periods or including only favorable costs. When comparing investments, risk is not factored in: a 30% ROI on a high-risk venture is not equivalent to 30% on a government bond. For cash flows occurring at different times, IRR or net present value (NPV) are more appropriate.

ROI vs IRR

IRR (Internal Rate of Return) addresses one of ROIâ€™s main weaknesses: the timing of cash flows. ROI treats all dollars equally regardless of when they occur. IRR solves for the discount rate that makes the net present value of all cash flows equal to zero. For a single lump-sum investment and return, ROI and IRR tell similar stories. For projects with multiple inflows and outflowsâ€”such as real estate with rental income and renovationsâ€”IRR provides a more accurate picture. See ROI vs IRR for a detailed comparison.

Common Mistakes

  • Comparing ROI across different time periods without annualizing
  • Ignoring fees, taxes, and transaction costs
  • Using ROI for investments with irregular cash flows instead of IRR
  • Confusing gross and net returnsâ€”ensure all costs are included
  • Extrapolating past performance as a guarantee of future results

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ROI?

ROI (Return on Investment) measures the profitability of an investment as a percentage of the initial cost. The formula is: ROI = [(Final Value âˆ’ Initial Investment) / Initial Investment] Ã— 100.

How do you calculate annualized ROI?

When can ROI be misleading?

ROI can be misleading when it ignores the time value of money, excludes costs like taxes or fees, or compares investments with different risk levels. Annualized ROI and IRR address some of these limitations.

What is a good ROI?

A "good" ROI depends on asset class, risk, and holding period. Stock market historical average is around 10% annually; real estate cap rates vary by market; business investments typically target 15â€“25% or higher depending on risk.

What is the difference between ROI and IRR?

ROI is a simple percentage gain over the total period. IRR (Internal Rate of Return) accounts for the timing of cash flows and finds the discount rate that makes NPV zero. IRR is better for investments with multiple inflows and outflows.
